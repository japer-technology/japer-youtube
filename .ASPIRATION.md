# japer-youtube aspiration

This project aspires to become a total YouTube experience for the super viewer.

The dream is simple: once you are logged into your YouTube account and your OpenAI account, your subscriptions should be processed into something that feels less like a feed and more like a personal pay TV service.

But the dream does not stop there. Taken to its edge, this becomes something larger: a personal media operating system where the viewer is the programmer, the scheduler, the network executive, and the audience — all at once.

## The core idea

Turn a YouTube subscription list into a channel-based viewing system.

Instead of asking the user to manually browse hundreds of creators, videos, playlists, and tabs, the product should organise everything into a clear television-style format:

- around 100 channels
- meaningful channel names
- grouped themes and genres
- specialised channels built from multiple subscriptions
- a viewing experience designed for continuous watching, not endless clicking

Taken further, the subscription list is only the seed. The system should grow a living content graph from it — mapping relationships between creators, topics, formats, upload rhythms, and the viewer's own behaviour. That graph becomes the nervous system of the entire experience. Every channel, every layout, every recommendation flows from it.

Eventually the viewer should not think about subscriptions at all. They should think about their network — the personal media universe that the content graph represents.

## What the experience should feel like

The goal is not just to show YouTube videos in a grid. The goal is to make YouTube feel programmable, curated, and alive.

The ideal experience includes:

- multiple screens
- multiple players on each screen
- fast switching between channels
- hover states that can control which video has sound
- options to play only the hover video
- options to mute everything except the active focus
- layouts that can be tuned for browsing, monitoring, or immersive viewing

This should feel like a control room, a modern TV wall, and a personal media centre built entirely from the viewer's subscriptions.

### Beyond the wall

But a TV wall is just the beginning. The experience should adapt to what the viewer is doing and what they need right now:

- a single focused player when the viewer wants depth
- a mosaic of silent thumbnails when the viewer wants to scan
- a ticker of titles and timestamps when the viewer wants awareness without commitment
- a background audio stream when the viewer is working and just wants to listen
- a cinematic mode that fills every pixel when the viewer is ready to be absorbed
- a companion mode that pairs a main video with related context, comments, or live chat alongside
- a dashboard mode that monitors several live streams at once, surfacing alerts when something interesting happens
- a lean-back mode that plays a curated sequence and asks nothing of the viewer at all

The interface should breathe. It should feel sparse when the viewer wants calm and dense when the viewer wants control. It should never feel like a website. It should feel like a place.

### Spatial and ambient

At the far edge, the experience extends beyond a single browser window:

- multiple browser windows that coordinate across monitors
- a tablet acting as a remote and companion screen
- a phone showing the now-playing card and queue
- spatial computing surfaces where channels float in the viewer's physical space
- ambient displays that show slow-rotating thumbnails or silent highlights as room decoration
- projection mapping that turns a wall into a living channel mosaic

The viewer's environment becomes the screen.

## Channel intelligence

Subscriptions should not remain as a flat list. They should be transformed into channels that make sense for the viewer.

That includes:

- channel naming controls
- manual and automatic grouping
- topic-based channels
- mood- or task-based channels
- temporary event channels
- favourites, highlights, and deep-dive channels
- channels for news, music, education, gaming, podcasts, live streams, or any other niche

OpenAI can help interpret subscription patterns, titles, metadata, upload cadence, and themes so that the system becomes easier to organise and easier to watch.

### The AI programme director

Taken to its edge, AI becomes more than an organiser. It becomes a programme director.

That means:

- generating a daily schedule: what to watch at breakfast, during lunch, in the evening wind-down
- building thematic blocks: a two-hour deep dive into a topic pulled from five different creators
- creating highlight reels: the best ten minutes from the last 24 hours of uploads across the whole network
- detecting emerging topics: when multiple subscriptions suddenly cover the same event, spin up a temporary breaking channel
- recognising creator arcs: when a creator starts a series, group the episodes and present them as a season
- predicting the viewer's interest: surface a video before the viewer searches for it, because the pattern of what they watch implies what they will want next
- composing narrative threads: weave videos from different creators into a coherent story about a single topic, ordered for understanding rather than chronology
- summarising what was missed: after a day away, present a concise briefing of what happened across the network, with links to go deeper on anything that matters

The AI should never feel like it is choosing for the viewer. It should feel like it understands the viewer and is making thoughtful suggestions that the viewer is always free to override.

### The content graph

Behind the channels sits a content graph — a living model of everything the viewer cares about.

Nodes in the graph:

- creators
- topics
- formats (tutorial, review, essay, vlog, stream, short, podcast)
- moods (calm, energetic, funny, serious, educational, comforting)
- series and playlists
- events and moments
- the viewer's own tags and annotations

Edges in the graph:

- creator-to-topic relationships
- cross-creator collaborations
- topic evolution over time
- the viewer's engagement patterns: what they finish, what they skip, what they return to

The content graph is the foundation for every intelligent feature. It powers channel generation, schedule building, recommendations, highlights, and the viewer's own exploration. It should be visible, explorable, and editable — the viewer should be able to see their own content universe mapped out and reshape it by hand when the AI gets it wrong.

## Viewer control

The viewer should have lots of options.

That means control over:

- channel names
- channel groups
- specialised channels
- sort order and priority
- layouts and screen density
- autoplay behaviour
- focus behaviour
- sound routing
- hover behaviour
- playback rules
- watch modes for discovery, passive viewing, deep focus, or background listening

The product should support both lightweight use and obsessive use.

### Deep control

Taken further, the viewer should be able to:

- write rules: "never autoplay videos longer than 20 minutes before noon"
- set quotas: "no more than 45 minutes of gaming content per day"
- define rituals: "every Sunday morning, build a two-hour playlist of calm long-form essays"
- pin triggers: "when a creator I follow goes live, bring that stream to the front of whatever layout I am in"
- create filters: "hide all shorts", "only show videos with captions", "skip anything uploaded more than 30 days ago"
- configure transitions: crossfade between videos, fade to black between channels, instant cut for fast switching
- assign keyboard shortcuts to any action: switch channel, mute, skip, save, rate, add to queue
- build macros: one key to mute all, switch to channel 7, and go full screen
- export and share configurations: hand a friend your entire channel setup as a file they can import

Control should scale from "I just want to watch" to "I want to program every detail of my viewing experience."

### Context-aware adaptation

The system should learn from context and adapt without being asked:

- time of day: quieter, slower content in the evening; energetic, short content in the morning
- day of week: weekday routines versus weekend exploration
- device: dense layouts on a desktop, simplified cards on a phone, ambient mode on a secondary display
- activity: background audio when the viewer is typing, full video when they are idle
- history: if the viewer always skips a particular creator's shorts but watches their long videos, stop surfacing the shorts
- velocity: if the viewer is switching rapidly, they are browsing — show more options; if they are lingering, they are watching — reduce distractions

The viewer should always be able to see what the system inferred and correct it. Adaptation should feel like a helpful assistant, never like a cage.

## Time intelligence

YouTube has no concept of the viewer's time. japer-youtube should.

That means:

- estimated watch time per channel, per session, per day
- a clock that says "you have watched 2 hours and 14 minutes today"
- schedule blocks: "I have 30 minutes — what fits?"
- time-shifted viewing: queue a live stream to start from the beginning while it is still going
- cadence awareness: know which creators upload daily, weekly, sporadically, or in bursts, and factor that into channel programming
- freshness signals: surface new uploads within minutes, but also resurface older gems that the viewer missed
- pacing: alternate between short and long content, between dense and light, between familiar and new, so the session has a rhythm rather than a blur
- a daily briefing: "here is what is new and what is worth your time today" as a single screen the viewer sees first
- a weekly digest: "here is what you watched, what you missed, and what is coming" as a reflective summary

The viewer's attention is finite. The product should respect that and help them spend it well.

## Content synthesis

YouTube is raw material. japer-youtube should refine it.

That means:

- AI-generated summaries of long videos: the key points in two paragraphs, so the viewer can decide whether to invest the full runtime
- chapter extraction: even when the creator has not added chapters, the AI should identify segments and let the viewer jump to what matters
- cross-video synthesis: after watching five videos about the same event, generate a unified briefing that captures what each creator contributed
- highlight reels: compress a three-hour live stream into the ten best moments
- transcript search: find the exact moment in any video where a specific thing was said
- audio extraction: pull the audio from any video and add it to a listening queue, turning video essays into podcasts
- translation overlays: watch a video in a language the viewer does not speak, with AI-generated subtitles or even dubbed audio
- creator briefs: a one-page profile of any subscription — what they make, how often, what topics, what their recent trajectory looks like
- annotation layers: the viewer's own notes, bookmarks, and highlights attached to specific moments in specific videos, searchable and revisitable

The viewer should never feel like they have to watch something just to know what it contains.

## Social and collaborative

Watching alone is the default, but watching together should be possible.

That means:

- shared channels: build a channel setup and share it as a link
- watch parties: synchronised playback with friends, each seeing the same video at the same time
- collaborative curation: a shared channel where multiple people contribute and vote on what plays next
- recommendations from friends: "my friend's network surfaced this — maybe I should watch it too"
- shared annotations: a friend's highlights on a video the viewer has not seen yet, acting as a preview
- public profiles: opt-in visibility of a viewer's channel setup, so others can discover interesting configurations
- community channels: channels built from the collective subscriptions and preferences of a group

Social features should be additive. The core experience is personal, private, and solo. Social is a layer that enhances without ever being required.

## Beyond YouTube

YouTube is the starting point, but the viewer's attention is not limited to one platform.

At the far edge:

- RSS feeds from blogs, newsletters, and podcasts woven into the channel grid alongside YouTube videos
- Twitch streams appearing as live channels alongside YouTube live streams
- podcast directories integrated so audio content is first-class
- Nebula, Curiosity Stream, or other video platforms blended into the same channel system
- local video files and personal recordings added to custom channels
- saved articles and bookmarks surfaced as text cards between video cards, creating a true media feed
- AI aggregation across all sources: the system knows the viewer's interests and pulls from everywhere, not just YouTube

The aspiration is not to replace YouTube. It is to put the viewer at the centre of all their media and let YouTube be the largest and most important source — but not the only one.

## The super viewer vision

This project should serve people who treat YouTube as their primary media platform.

For that viewer, the application should eventually become:

- a better way to follow subscriptions
- a better way to discover what matters right now
- a better way to keep many streams of interest visible at once
- a better way to move between casual watching and deliberate watching
- a better way to turn chaos into channels

And beyond that:

- a personal broadcast network where the viewer is the programmer
- a living archive of everything they have watched, annotated, and valued
- an AI partner that understands their taste and respects their time
- a control surface that adapts to their mood, their device, and their moment
- a media operating system that grows more useful the more it is used
- a place that feels like it was built for them and no one else

The super viewer is not passive. They are a power user of attention. They have many subscriptions, strong opinions about what they watch, limited time, and high expectations. They do not want an algorithm to decide for them. They want tools sharp enough to let them decide faster and better.

japer-youtube should be the instrument they play.

## Long-term aspiration

japer-youtube should grow into a full personal YouTube operating layer:

- authenticated
- personalised
- channelised
- AI-assisted
- highly configurable
- designed for power users

And at its furthest horizon:

- predictive: anticipating the viewer's needs before they articulate them
- generative: creating new experiences from raw content — summaries, highlights, compilations, schedules
- composable: every feature is a building block the viewer can rearrange
- extensible: an open architecture where new sources, new layouts, new AI capabilities can be added without breaking what exists
- portable: the viewer's configuration, history, and preferences belong to them, exportable and transferable
- persistent: the system remembers everything — every video watched, every channel tuned, every preference expressed — and uses that memory to get better over time
- respectful: the viewer's data is their own, their attention is valued, and the system never optimises for engagement over wellbeing

The final aspiration is a complete media environment where the viewer can shape their own network, their own screens, their own schedule, and their own flow of attention — not just for YouTube, but for everything they watch, hear, and read.

In short: personal pay TV, built from YouTube, expanded to the edge of reality, for the super viewer.
