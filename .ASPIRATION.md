# japer-youtube aspiration

This project aspires to become a total YouTube experience for the super viewer.

The dream is simple: once you are logged into your YouTube account and your OpenAI account, your subscriptions should be processed into something that feels less like a feed and more like a personal pay TV service.

But the dream does not stop there. Taken to its edge, this becomes something larger: a personal media operating system where the viewer is the programmer, the scheduler, the network executive, and the audience — all at once.

## The core idea

Turn a YouTube subscription list into a channel-based viewing system.

Instead of asking the user to manually browse hundreds of creators, videos, playlists, and tabs, the product should organise everything into a clear television-style format:

- around 100 channels
- meaningful channel names
- grouped themes and genres
- specialised channels built from multiple subscriptions
- a viewing experience designed for continuous watching, not endless clicking

Taken further, the subscription list is only the seed. The system should grow a living content graph from it — mapping relationships between creators, topics, formats, upload rhythms, and the viewer's own behaviour. That graph becomes the nervous system of the entire experience. Every channel, every layout, every recommendation flows from it.

Eventually the viewer should not think about subscriptions at all. They should think about their network — the personal media universe that the content graph represents.

## What the experience should feel like

The goal is not just to show YouTube videos in a grid. The goal is to make YouTube feel programmable, curated, and alive.

The ideal experience includes:

- multiple screens
- multiple players on each screen
- fast switching between channels
- hover states that can control which video has sound
- options to play only the hover video
- options to mute everything except the active focus
- layouts that can be tuned for browsing, monitoring, or immersive viewing

This should feel like a control room, a modern TV wall, and a personal media centre built entirely from the viewer's subscriptions.

### Beyond the wall

But a TV wall is just the beginning. The experience should adapt to what the viewer is doing and what they need right now:

- a single focused player when the viewer wants depth
- a mosaic of silent thumbnails when the viewer wants to scan
- a ticker of titles and timestamps when the viewer wants awareness without commitment
- a background audio stream when the viewer is working and just wants to listen
- a cinematic mode that fills every pixel when the viewer is ready to be absorbed
- a companion mode that pairs a main video with related context, comments, or live chat alongside
- a dashboard mode that monitors several live streams at once, surfacing alerts when something interesting happens
- a lean-back mode that plays a curated sequence and asks nothing of the viewer at all

The interface should breathe. It should feel sparse when the viewer wants calm and dense when the viewer wants control. It should never feel like a website. It should feel like a place.

### Spatial and ambient

At the far edge, the experience extends beyond a single browser window:

- multiple browser windows that coordinate across monitors
- a tablet acting as a remote and companion screen
- a phone showing the now-playing card and queue
- spatial computing surfaces where channels float in the viewer's physical space
- ambient displays that show slow-rotating thumbnails or silent highlights as room decoration
- projection mapping that turns a wall into a living channel mosaic

The viewer's environment becomes the screen.

## Channel intelligence

Subscriptions should not remain as a flat list. They should be transformed into channels that make sense for the viewer.

That includes:

- channel naming controls
- manual and automatic grouping
- topic-based channels
- mood- or task-based channels
- temporary event channels
- favourites, highlights, and deep-dive channels
- channels for news, music, education, gaming, podcasts, live streams, or any other niche

OpenAI can help interpret subscription patterns, titles, metadata, upload cadence, and themes so that the system becomes easier to organise and easier to watch.

### The AI programme director

Taken to its edge, AI becomes more than an organiser. It becomes a programme director.

That means:

- generating a daily schedule: what to watch at breakfast, during lunch, in the evening wind-down
- building thematic blocks: a two-hour deep dive into a topic pulled from five different creators
- creating highlight reels: the best ten minutes from the last 24 hours of uploads across the whole network
- detecting emerging topics: when multiple subscriptions suddenly cover the same event, spin up a temporary breaking channel
- recognising creator arcs: when a creator starts a series, group the episodes and present them as a season
- predicting the viewer's interest: surface a video before the viewer searches for it, because the pattern of what they watch implies what they will want next
- composing narrative threads: weave videos from different creators into a coherent story about a single topic, ordered for understanding rather than chronology
- summarising what was missed: after a day away, present a concise briefing of what happened across the network, with links to go deeper on anything that matters

The AI should never feel like it is choosing for the viewer. It should feel like it understands the viewer and is making thoughtful suggestions that the viewer is always free to override.

### The content graph

Behind the channels sits a content graph — a living model of everything the viewer cares about.

Nodes in the graph:

- creators
- topics
- formats (tutorial, review, essay, vlog, stream, short, podcast)
- moods (calm, energetic, funny, serious, educational, comforting)
- series and playlists
- events and moments
- the viewer's own tags and annotations

Edges in the graph:

- creator-to-topic relationships
- cross-creator collaborations
- topic evolution over time
- the viewer's engagement patterns: what they finish, what they skip, what they return to

The content graph is the foundation for every intelligent feature. It powers channel generation, schedule building, recommendations, highlights, and the viewer's own exploration. It should be visible, explorable, and editable — the viewer should be able to see their own content universe mapped out and reshape it by hand when the AI gets it wrong.

## Viewer control

The viewer should have lots of options.

That means control over:

- channel names
- channel groups
- specialised channels
- sort order and priority
- layouts and screen density
- autoplay behaviour
- focus behaviour
- sound routing
- hover behaviour
- playback rules
- watch modes for discovery, passive viewing, deep focus, or background listening

The product should support both lightweight use and obsessive use.

### Deep control

Taken further, the viewer should be able to:

- write rules: "never autoplay videos longer than 20 minutes before noon"
- set quotas: "no more than 45 minutes of gaming content per day"
- define rituals: "every Sunday morning, build a two-hour playlist of calm long-form essays"
- pin triggers: "when a creator I follow goes live, bring that stream to the front of whatever layout I am in"
- create filters: "hide all shorts", "only show videos with captions", "skip anything uploaded more than 30 days ago"
- configure transitions: crossfade between videos, fade to black between channels, instant cut for fast switching
- assign keyboard shortcuts to any action: switch channel, mute, skip, save, rate, add to queue
- build macros: one key to mute all, switch to channel 7, and go full screen
- export and share configurations: hand a friend your entire channel setup as a file they can import

Control should scale from "I just want to watch" to "I want to program every detail of my viewing experience."

### Context-aware adaptation

The system should learn from context and adapt without being asked:

- time of day: quieter, slower content in the evening; energetic, short content in the morning
- day of week: weekday routines versus weekend exploration
- device: dense layouts on a desktop, simplified cards on a phone, ambient mode on a secondary display
- activity: background audio when the viewer is typing, full video when they are idle
- history: if the viewer always skips a particular creator's shorts but watches their long videos, stop surfacing the shorts
- velocity: if the viewer is switching rapidly, they are browsing — show more options; if they are lingering, they are watching — reduce distractions

The viewer should always be able to see what the system inferred and correct it. Adaptation should feel like a helpful assistant, never like a cage.

## Time intelligence

YouTube has no concept of the viewer's time. japer-youtube should.

That means:

- estimated watch time per channel, per session, per day
- a clock that says "you have watched 2 hours and 14 minutes today"
- schedule blocks: "I have 30 minutes — what fits?"
- time-shifted viewing: queue a live stream to start from the beginning while it is still going
- cadence awareness: know which creators upload daily, weekly, sporadically, or in bursts, and factor that into channel programming
- freshness signals: surface new uploads within minutes, but also resurface older gems that the viewer missed
- pacing: alternate between short and long content, between dense and light, between familiar and new, so the session has a rhythm rather than a blur
- a daily briefing: "here is what is new and what is worth your time today" as a single screen the viewer sees first
- a weekly digest: "here is what you watched, what you missed, and what is coming" as a reflective summary

The viewer's attention is finite. The product should respect that and help them spend it well.

## Content synthesis

YouTube is raw material. japer-youtube should refine it.

That means:

- AI-generated summaries of long videos: the key points in two paragraphs, so the viewer can decide whether to invest the full runtime
- chapter extraction: even when the creator has not added chapters, the AI should identify segments and let the viewer jump to what matters
- cross-video synthesis: after watching five videos about the same event, generate a unified briefing that captures what each creator contributed
- highlight reels: compress a three-hour live stream into the ten best moments
- transcript search: find the exact moment in any video where a specific thing was said
- audio extraction: pull the audio from any video and add it to a listening queue, turning video essays into podcasts
- translation overlays: watch a video in a language the viewer does not speak, with AI-generated subtitles or even dubbed audio
- creator briefs: a one-page profile of any subscription — what they make, how often, what topics, what their recent trajectory looks like
- annotation layers: the viewer's own notes, bookmarks, and highlights attached to specific moments in specific videos, searchable and revisitable

The viewer should never feel like they have to watch something just to know what it contains.

## Social and collaborative

Watching alone is the default, but watching together should be possible.

That means:

- shared channels: build a channel setup and share it as a link
- watch parties: synchronised playback with friends, each seeing the same video at the same time
- collaborative curation: a shared channel where multiple people contribute and vote on what plays next
- recommendations from friends: "my friend's network surfaced this — maybe I should watch it too"
- shared annotations: a friend's highlights on a video the viewer has not seen yet, acting as a preview
- public profiles: opt-in visibility of a viewer's channel setup, so others can discover interesting configurations
- community channels: channels built from the collective subscriptions and preferences of a group

Social features should be additive. The core experience is personal, private, and solo. Social is a layer that enhances without ever being required.

## Beyond YouTube

YouTube is the starting point, but the viewer's attention is not limited to one platform.

At the far edge:

- RSS feeds from blogs, newsletters, and podcasts woven into the channel grid alongside YouTube videos
- Twitch streams appearing as live channels alongside YouTube live streams
- podcast directories integrated so audio content is first-class
- Nebula, Curiosity Stream, or other video platforms blended into the same channel system
- local video files and personal recordings added to custom channels
- saved articles and bookmarks surfaced as text cards between video cards, creating a true media feed
- AI aggregation across all sources: the system knows the viewer's interests and pulls from everywhere, not just YouTube

The aspiration is not to replace YouTube. It is to put the viewer at the centre of all their media and let YouTube be the largest and most important source — but not the only one.

## How the magic happens

Everything above describes a destination. This section describes the road — how the current codebase evolves, what technologies carry each feature, and how the pieces connect.

### Architecture evolution

The current codebase is a single-page application assembled from modular files under `src/`. Seven JavaScript IIFE modules, four HTML fragments, and eight CSS files are stitched together by `build.js` into one self-contained `dist/index.html`. There are zero npm dependencies. That simplicity is a strength and should be preserved as long as possible.

The architecture evolves in layers:

**Layer 0 — what exists today.**
Vanilla JavaScript modules namespaced under `window.JaperYT`. OAuth 2.0 via Google Identity Services. YouTube Data API v3 for subscriptions, activities, and search. A sidebar, a video grid, and an overlay player. Everything runs client-side. No server.

**Layer 1 — local intelligence.**
IndexedDB becomes the persistent memory. Every subscription, every video metadata record, every viewer action is written to a local store. The content graph lives here. The app can boot without a network call because yesterday's state is still on disk. A lightweight reactive layer — possibly just custom events or a tiny observable pattern — replaces the current manual DOM updates, so that when the content graph changes, the UI reflects it automatically.

**Layer 2 — AI integration.**
A thin configuration surface lets the viewer provide an OpenAI API key. The app makes direct client-side calls to the OpenAI API — no backend server required. A prompt layer sits between the raw data and the AI: it formats subscription metadata, video titles, upload cadences, and viewer history into structured prompts, and parses the responses into channel definitions, schedules, summaries, and recommendations. The AI never touches raw credentials or tokens. It only sees content metadata.

**Layer 3 — multi-surface.**
The single browser tab becomes a coordinator. The BroadcastChannel API lets multiple browser windows share state — one window shows the control room layout, another shows a focused player, a third shows the content graph explorer. A service worker manages caching and background sync. For tablet and phone companion screens, a lightweight WebSocket relay (or WebRTC data channel) bridges devices on the same local network.

**Layer 4 — extensibility.**
A plugin architecture allows new content sources, new layout modes, and new AI capabilities to be added as self-contained modules. Each plugin registers with a manifest that declares what it provides (a source, a layout, a synthesis function) and the core system wires it in. The viewer can enable, disable, and configure plugins through a settings panel.

Throughout all layers, the zero-dependency instinct remains. External libraries are adopted only when building the equivalent from scratch would be unreliable or prohibitively complex — for example, a WebRTC signalling library, or a proper IndexedDB wrapper. The build stays simple: `node build.js` should always work.

### The content graph — data model

The content graph is the nervous system. It is not a relational database. It is a lightweight in-memory graph that persists to IndexedDB and can be exported as JSON.

**Nodes** are typed objects:

```
Creator    { id, name, thumbnailUrl, uploadCadence, topics[], formats[] }
Video      { id, title, creatorId, publishedAt, duration, topics[], format, mood }
Topic      { id, label, parentTopicId? }
Format     { id, label }                          // tutorial, essay, vlog, stream, short, podcast
Mood       { id, label }                          // calm, energetic, funny, serious, educational
Series     { id, label, videoIds[] }
Channel    { id, name, sourceRule, memberCreatorIds[], memberTopicIds[], pinned }
Tag        { id, label, viewerDefined: true }
```

**Edges** are typed relationships:

```
CREATES        Creator  → Video
COVERS         Video    → Topic
HAS_FORMAT     Video    → Format
HAS_MOOD       Video    → Mood
BELONGS_TO     Video    → Series
COLLABORATES   Creator  → Creator     (detected from co-appearances, shared topics)
EVOLVES_INTO   Topic    → Topic       (topic evolution over time)
WATCHED        Viewer   → Video       { timestamp, completionPercent, rating? }
SKIPPED        Viewer   → Video       { timestamp }
QUEUED         Viewer   → Video       { addedAt, position }
TAGGED         Viewer   → Video|Creator|Topic  { tagId }
```

The graph is built incrementally. When subscriptions load, `Creator` nodes are upserted. When videos load, `Video` nodes are created and edges are inferred from titles and metadata. When the viewer watches or skips, engagement edges are recorded. Over time, the graph becomes a dense map of the viewer's content universe.

AI augments the graph. When the viewer requests channel organisation, the prompt layer serialises a subgraph — creator names, topic labels, recent upload titles — and sends it to the AI. The AI returns proposed `Channel` definitions with source rules like "all creators who cover topic X" or "these three specific creators." The viewer confirms, edits, or rejects. Confirmed channels become nodes in the graph.

A simple query interface lets the rest of the app traverse the graph:

```javascript
graph.nodes('Creator', { topic: 'technology' })      // creators covering tech
graph.edges('WATCHED', { since: '7d', minCompletion: 0.8 })  // fully watched this week
graph.neighbours('Creator', creatorId, 'COLLABORATES')        // collaborators
graph.path('Topic', fromId, toId)                              // topic evolution chain
```

This interface powers every intelligent feature: channel generation, schedule building, highlight selection, recommendation ranking, and the viewer's own exploration UI.

### AI integration layer

The AI is not a black box. It is a structured pipeline with visible inputs, inspectable outputs, and viewer override at every step.

**How it connects.** The viewer enters an OpenAI API key in settings. The key is stored in the browser's credential store (or encrypted in IndexedDB). All AI calls are made directly from the client using the OpenAI chat completions API. No intermediate server. The viewer's data never leaves their machine except as prompt text sent to OpenAI.

**Prompt patterns.** Each AI feature uses a dedicated prompt template:

- **Channel generation.** Input: a list of creator names, their topics, their upload frequency. Output: a JSON array of proposed channels, each with a name, a description, and a list of member creators. The prompt instructs the AI to think like a TV network programmer — group by theme, create crossover channels, name them clearly.

- **Schedule building.** Input: the viewer's channel list, current time of day, available duration, and recent watch history. Output: an ordered playlist with estimated durations and transition notes. The prompt instructs the AI to balance variety, freshness, and the viewer's demonstrated preferences.

- **Summarisation.** Input: a video transcript (fetched via YouTube's timed text API or a third-party caption source). Output: a structured summary — key points, notable quotes, a one-sentence verdict. The prompt instructs the AI to be concise and neutral.

- **Cross-video synthesis.** Input: summaries of multiple videos on the same topic. Output: a unified briefing that attributes each point to its source creator. The prompt instructs the AI to identify agreement, disagreement, and unique contributions.

- **Breaking topic detection.** Input: the last 24 hours of video titles and descriptions across all subscriptions. Output: a list of topics that multiple creators have suddenly covered, ranked by convergence. The prompt instructs the AI to distinguish genuine events from coincidence.

**Caching and cost control.** AI responses are cached in IndexedDB keyed by a hash of the prompt. Identical requests return cached results. The viewer can set a monthly token budget. The app tracks cumulative usage and warns when the budget is nearly exhausted. Expensive operations (full-transcript summarisation, cross-video synthesis) require explicit viewer confirmation before running.

**Fallback without AI.** Every AI-powered feature has a non-AI fallback. Channel generation falls back to alphabetical grouping. Scheduling falls back to reverse-chronological ordering. Summarisation falls back to showing the video description. The product should always be useful without an OpenAI key. AI makes it better. It never makes it necessary.

### Multi-screen rendering engine

The current app has one layout: sidebar plus grid. The aspiration demands many layouts, many players, many screens.

**Layout system.** CSS Grid becomes the backbone. A layout is defined as a JSON object:

```json
{
  "name": "control-room",
  "columns": 4,
  "rows": 3,
  "cells": [
    { "id": "main",    "col": "1 / 4", "row": "1 / 3", "player": true },
    { "id": "pip-1",   "col": "4 / 5", "row": "1 / 2", "player": true },
    { "id": "pip-2",   "col": "4 / 5", "row": "2 / 3", "player": true },
    { "id": "ticker",  "col": "1 / 5", "row": "3 / 4", "player": false, "widget": "ticker" }
  ]
}
```

The rendering engine reads this definition and generates a CSS Grid container with named areas. Each cell can host a player (YouTube iframe), a widget (ticker, sidebar, graph explorer), or empty space. The viewer can create, edit, save, and switch between layouts through a visual editor or by writing JSON directly.

**Multiple players.** YouTube's iframe API allows multiple embeds on the same page. Each cell marked `player: true` gets its own iframe. A player manager tracks which cells are active, which video is playing in each, and which cell has audio focus. Only one cell has unmuted audio at any time (unless the viewer explicitly overrides).

**Sound routing.** The Web Audio API captures audio from each player via `createMediaElementSource` (where browser policy allows) and routes it through gain nodes. The viewer can:

- mute all except one cell
- set per-cell volume levels
- hover to temporarily solo a cell's audio
- crossfade audio when switching focus

Where `createMediaElementSource` is blocked by cross-origin restrictions on YouTube iframes, the fallback is simpler: mute/unmute via the iframe API's `mute()` and `unMute()` methods. Less elegant, but functional.

**Multi-window coordination.** The BroadcastChannel API enables this pattern:

1. The viewer opens a second browser window pointing to the same app.
2. Both windows detect each other via a shared BroadcastChannel named `japer-sync`.
3. One window becomes the "controller" (shows the sidebar, the channel list, the content graph). The other becomes a "display" (shows only the player grid).
4. Commands flow from the controller to the display: "play video X in cell 2", "switch layout to cinematic", "mute all."
5. State flows back: "cell 2 finished playback", "viewer clicked next."

For cross-device coordination (tablet as remote, phone as now-playing card), a lightweight WebSocket relay running on `localhost` or a local network discovery protocol handles the bridge. This is the one place where a small local server process becomes necessary — a Node.js script that relays messages between devices on the same Wi-Fi.

### The rules engine

Deep viewer control requires a rules engine. Not a programming language — a structured set of conditions and actions that the viewer defines through a form UI or a simple text syntax.

**Rule structure:**

```
WHEN  <condition>
THEN  <action>
```

**Conditions** can be:

- time-based: `time.hour < 12`, `day.isWeekend`
- content-based: `video.duration > 20m`, `video.format == 'short'`, `creator.name == 'X'`
- context-based: `session.watchedMinutes > 60`, `channel.current == 'gaming'`
- event-based: `creator.wentLive`, `video.justUploaded`

**Actions** can be:

- `hide` — remove from feed
- `prioritise` — boost to top
- `autoplay false` — do not autoplay
- `route to <channel>` — move to a specific channel
- `notify` — show a toast
- `switch layout <name>` — change the layout
- `mute all` — silence everything
- `queue <video>` — add to watch queue

**Examples in text syntax:**

```
WHEN time.hour < 12 AND video.duration > 20m THEN autoplay false
WHEN creator.wentLive AND creator.favourite THEN notify AND switch layout "single-focus"
WHEN session.watchedMinutes > 90 THEN notify "You have been watching for 90 minutes"
WHEN video.format == "short" THEN hide
```

The rules engine evaluates conditions against the content graph and the current session state. Rules are stored as JSON in IndexedDB and can be exported, imported, and shared.

A visual rule builder presents conditions as dropdowns and sliders and actions as buttons, so the viewer never has to write text syntax unless they want to.

### Time intelligence

Time intelligence is built from three data sources:

1. **Upload cadence analysis.** The app records timestamps of every video it sees from each creator. A simple statistical model (median interval, variance) classifies creators as daily, weekly, biweekly, sporadic, or bursty. This powers the "what is new and what is expected" signals.

2. **Watch time tracking.** Every time a video plays, the app records start time, pause events, seek events, and completion. Total watch time per session, per day, per week is computed from these records. The player module emits events; the time intelligence module listens and writes to IndexedDB.

3. **Schedule generation.** Given the viewer's available time ("I have 30 minutes"), the system queries the content graph for unwatched videos, ranks them by freshness, viewer affinity, and duration fit, and assembles a playlist that fills the time window. AI can improve the ranking by considering mood, topic balance, and narrative flow, but the basic version works with simple heuristics.

**The daily briefing** is a computed view: new uploads since last visit, sorted by the viewer's engagement history with each creator. Creators the viewer always watches are at the top. Creators the viewer sometimes skips are lower. Creators the viewer hasn't watched in weeks are in a "you might have missed" section. The briefing is generated locally from the content graph — no AI call required for the basic version.

**The weekly digest** adds reflection: total hours watched, top creators by watch time, topics explored, videos completed versus abandoned, and a "this week versus last week" comparison. This is pure local computation over the IndexedDB records.

### Content synthesis pipeline

Content synthesis is the most AI-intensive feature set. Each capability has a specific data flow:

**Transcript access.** YouTube exposes timed text tracks (captions) via the `timedtext` API endpoint. For videos with auto-generated or creator-provided captions, the app fetches the full transcript text. This raw text becomes the input for summarisation, chapter extraction, and search.

**Summarisation.** The transcript (or the video description if no transcript is available) is sent to the OpenAI API with a summarisation prompt. The response is cached alongside the video node in the content graph. The viewer sees a "Summary" tab on the video card — two paragraphs and a list of key points.

**Chapter extraction.** When the creator has not provided chapters, the AI analyses the transcript for topic shifts. It returns a list of timestamps and labels. The app overlays these as clickable markers on the player's progress bar using the YouTube iframe API's `seekTo` method.

**Cross-video synthesis.** When the content graph detects multiple recent videos covering the same topic (via title keyword overlap or AI-detected topic convergence), the viewer can request a synthesis. The app fetches summaries for all relevant videos, sends them as a batch to the AI, and receives a unified briefing. Each point is attributed to its source video with a clickable link.

**Highlight extraction.** For long-form content (streams, podcasts), the AI identifies high-engagement segments based on transcript markers: exclamations, topic changes, audience reactions (from chat if available). The app presents a "highlights" timeline the viewer can scrub through.

**Transcript search.** All fetched transcripts are indexed locally using a simple inverted index stored in IndexedDB. The viewer types a phrase and the app returns matching videos with exact timestamps. Clicking a result opens the player at that moment.

**Audio extraction.** The app can create a "listening queue" that plays videos with the screen off or in a minimised tab. The YouTube iframe API supports audio-only playback by simply hiding the iframe or playing in a background tab. The UI presents this as a podcast-style player with a play/pause button, progress bar, and skip controls.

### Persistence and portability

Everything the viewer creates — channels, rules, layouts, tags, watch history, the entire content graph — lives in IndexedDB. This means:

- **Offline capability.** After the first load, the app can boot from local data. New API calls fetch updates, but the viewer can browse their channels, review their history, and read cached summaries without a network connection.

- **Export.** A single "Export" button serialises the full state to a JSON file. This file contains channel definitions, rules, layouts, graph nodes and edges, and preferences. No credentials or tokens are included.

- **Import.** Dragging a JSON file onto the app (or selecting it via a file picker) restores the full state. This enables backup, migration between machines, and sharing configurations with other viewers.

- **Selective sharing.** The viewer can export just their channel configuration (without watch history) as a shareable file. A friend can import it and get the same channel structure applied to their own subscriptions.

A future sync layer could use a viewer-controlled backend (a personal server, a cloud storage bucket, or a peer-to-peer protocol) to keep multiple devices in sync. But the default is local-first. The viewer's data stays on their machine until they explicitly choose to move it.

### How the phases unfold

The aspiration is vast. It does not arrive all at once. It unfolds in phases, each one building on the last, each one useful on its own.

**Phase 1 — enhanced single-page app.**
Build on the current MVP. Add IndexedDB persistence. Add the content graph data model. Add watch time tracking. Add layout switching (single player, grid, cinematic). Add keyboard shortcuts. Add a settings panel for API keys. The viewer gets a better version of what exists: faster, stateful, more controllable.

**Phase 2 — AI-powered channel organisation.**
Integrate the OpenAI API. Build the prompt layer. Implement channel generation from subscriptions. Add the channel editor. Add the daily briefing view. The viewer goes from a flat subscription list to a personal channel lineup — the core transformation.

**Phase 3 — multi-screen control room.**
